Introduction

Why Study Statistics?

Statistics is woven into nearly every domain of modern life — sports analytics, public health surveillance, economic forecasting, clinical drug trials, and even the algorithms behind social media feeds. Whenever you read that “30 minutes of exercise three times a week raises HDL cholesterol by 10–15%” or that “the median credit-card debt for college students is $3,173,” you are encountering a statistical claim.

Definition: Statistics

Statistics is the science of conducting studies to collect, organise, summarise, analyse, and draw conclusions from data.

Three reasons every student should learn statistics

  1. Reading research critically — Every profession produces statistical reports. Understanding the vocabulary and logic of statistical studies allows you to evaluate whether a claim is well-supported or misleading.

  2. Conducting your own research — Designing experiments, gathering data, analysing results, and communicating findings honestly are skills rooted in statistical thinking.

  3. Being an informed citizen and consumer — From interpreting government budgets to evaluating product safety claims, statistical reasoning empowers better everyday decisions.

Core Vocabulary

Before exploring the two branches of statistics, three foundational terms must be defined.

Definition: Variable

A variable is any characteristic or attribute that can assume different values across individuals, objects, or time points.

Definition: Data

Data are the specific values — measurements or observations — that a variable takes on. A collection of such values is called a data set; each individual entry is a datum. When the value a variable takes is governed by chance, the variable is called a random variable.

Section 1–1

Descriptive and Inferential Statistics

All statistical work falls into one of two broad branches, distinguished by what the analyst is trying to accomplish with the data.

Definition: Descriptive Statistics

Descriptive statistics encompasses the methods used to collect, organise, summarise, and present data in a clear and informative form — through tables, charts, averages, and other numerical measures. The goal is to describe what the data show, without extending conclusions beyond the data set itself.

Definition: Inferential Statistics

Inferential statistics uses information gathered from a sample to draw conclusions (inferences) about a larger population. It relies on probability theory and includes estimation, hypothesis testing, identifying relationships among variables, and making predictions.

The two branches in practice

Branch Goal Typical output
Descriptive Summarise the data at hand Mean, median, table, chart
Inferential Generalise to a population Confidence interval, p-value

Example — Descriptive: The U.S. Census Bureau surveys all residents every 10 years and reports the average household income, age distribution, and educational attainment. These summaries describe the population directly from a complete count.

Example — Inferential: A pharmaceutical company tests a new pain reliever on 200 volunteers and concludes that it is effective for all adults — a generalisation beyond the trial participants.

Population and Sample

Definition: Population

A population consists of all subjects — people, objects, or events — that belong to the group under investigation. Populations can be finite (all students enrolled at a university) or theoretically infinite (all possible coin flips).

Definition: Sample

A sample is a subset of subjects selected from the population for the purpose of study. A properly chosen sample should possess characteristics similar to those of the full population, allowing valid inferences to be drawn.

Why use a sample instead of a census?

Studying an entire population is often:

  • Too expensive — surveying every household nationwide
  • Too time-consuming — testing every manufactured item for defects
  • Physically impossible — measuring every star in a galaxy
  • Destructive — testing every firework to see if it ignites

A carefully designed sample provides nearly as much information as a full census at a fraction of the cost.

Section 1–2

Variables and Types of Data

Variables are classified along two dimensions: their nature (qualitative vs. quantitative) and, for quantitative variables, their continuity (discrete vs. continuous).

Definition: Qualitative Variable

A qualitative (or categorical) variable places each observation into a non-numerical category defined by some attribute or characteristic. Examples: gender, blood type, political affiliation, eye colour.

Definition: Quantitative Variable

A quantitative variable is inherently numerical and allows meaningful arithmetic operations. Observations can be ordered and compared by magnitude. Examples: age, height, income, temperature.

Discrete and Continuous Variables

Definition: Discrete Variable

A discrete variable can assume only isolated, countable values — typically non-negative integers. There are no possible values between consecutive counts. Examples: number of children in a family, number of defective items in a shipment, number of emergency calls per hour.

Definition: Continuous Variable

A continuous variable can assume any value within an interval — including fractions and decimals — because it is obtained by measurement rather than counting. In principle, infinitely many values exist between any two observations. Examples: height, weight, temperature, elapsed time.

Boundaries of Continuous Data

Because continuous measurements are always rounded to the nearest unit of precision, every recorded value represents an interval of possible true values. These intervals are called class boundaries.

Rule: Computing Boundaries

Boundaries are found by subtracting and adding half of one unit of precision from the recorded value. If data are recorded to whole numbers → boundaries extend by ±0.5 If data are recorded to tenths → boundaries extend by ±0.05

Boundary examples

Variable Recorded Value Boundaries
Length 15 cm 14.5 – 15.5 cm
Temperature 86 °F 85.5 – 86.5 °F
Time 0.43 sec 0.425 – 0.435 sec
Mass 1.6 g 1.55 – 1.65 g

Levels of Measurement

Beyond qualitative/quantitative, variables are characterised by four measurement scales that determine which mathematical operations and statistical procedures are appropriate.

Level Properties Example
Nominal Categories only; no order Eye colour, zip code
Ordinal Ordered categories; gaps unequal Letter grade (A > B > C …)
Interval Equal spacing; no true zero Temperature (°F), IQ
Ratio Equal spacing + true zero Height, weight, income

Each higher level inherits all properties of the levels below it.

Definition: Nominal Level

The nominal level classifies data into mutually exclusive, exhaustive categories in which no ordering or ranking can be meaningfully imposed. Numbers may be used as labels (e.g., jersey numbers, zip codes), but arithmetic operations on them are meaningless.

Definition: Ordinal Level

The ordinal level classifies data into categories that can be ranked, but the differences between ranks are not guaranteed to be equal. Knowing that “Excellent” > “Good” > “Poor” does not reveal how much better one rating is than another.

Definition: Interval Level

The interval level has all ordinal properties plus equal, meaningful spacing between units. However, there is no true zero point — a score of 0 does not mean complete absence of the attribute. Therefore, ratios are not interpretable (80°F is not “twice as warm” as 40°F).

Definition: Ratio Level

The ratio level possesses all interval properties plus a genuine zero point, making ratios meaningful and interpretable. A person earning $80,000 earns twice as much as one earning $40,000. A weight of 0 kg means no weight at all.

Section 1–3

Data Collection and Sampling Techniques

Researchers gather data in several ways: telephone surveys, mailed questionnaires, personal interviews, direct observation, and examination of existing records. Each approach involves a trade-off of cost, accuracy, coverage, and potential for bias.

Common survey methods

Method Advantage Disadvantage
Telephone survey Fast; inexpensive Misses unlisted numbers; interviewer tone can bias
Mailed questionnaire Wide geographic reach; anonymous Low response rate; literacy barriers
Personal interview In-depth responses Costly; interviewer selection bias

Why Sampling Matters

Gathering data from every member of a population (a census) is rarely feasible. Instead, researchers rely on samples — and the method used to draw those samples determines whether the results can be trusted.

A sample drawn haphazardly introduces bias: systematically favoring certain kinds of subjects while excluding others. Surveying only mall shoppers on a Tuesday morning excludes working adults, shift workers, home-makers, and many others.

The solution is to use a probability-based sampling method that gives every member of the population a calculable, non-zero chance of being selected.

Definition: Random Sampling

In simple random sampling, every possible sample of the desired size has an equal probability of being selected. Subjects are numbered, and a computer or random-number table is used to choose which numbers are included.

Definition: Systematic Sampling

In systematic sampling, subjects are numbered from 1 to N. A starting point is chosen at random between 1 and k (where k = N ÷ n, the desired sample size), and then every kth subject is selected thereafter.

Definition: Stratified Sampling

In stratified sampling, the population is first divided into non-overlapping, exhaustive subgroups called strata (based on a characteristic relevant to the study). An independent random sample is then drawn from each stratum, ensuring all important subgroups are represented.

Definition: Cluster Sampling

In cluster sampling, the population is divided into naturally occurring clusters (e.g., city blocks, hospital wards, school classes). A random selection of clusters is made, and all members of the chosen clusters form the sample. Most useful when the population is geographically spread out.

Section 1–4

Observational and Experimental Studies

All statistical studies can be categorised by how the researcher relates to the data-generating process.

Definition: Observational Study

In an observational study, the researcher watches, records, and analyses events without intervening or manipulating any variable. Because no variable is controlled, cause-and-effect relationships cannot be established — only associations can be noted.

Definition: Experimental Study

In an experimental study, the researcher actively manipulates at least one variable and measures the effect on another. Properly designed experiments — with random assignment and control groups — permit causal conclusions.

Key Components of an Experiment

Definition: Independent Variable (Explanatory Variable)

The independent variable is the factor the researcher deliberately changes or controls. It is the presumed cause in a cause-and-effect relationship.

Definition: Dependent Variable (Outcome Variable)

The dependent variable is the response that is measured to determine whether the manipulation had an effect. It is the presumed effect.

Definition: Confounding Variable

A confounding variable is one that influences the dependent variable but was not separated from the independent variable — making it impossible to determine which factor drove the observed change.

Example — The Sit-Up Study

The Sit-Up Study (Virginia Polytechnic Institute)

Female undergraduates were randomly divided into two groups and performed as many sit-ups as possible in 90 seconds, once per day for four days.

  • Control group: told only to “do your best”
  • Treatment group: told to increase their daily count by 10%

Independent variable: type of instruction (vague vs. specific goal) Dependent variable: number of sit-ups completed per session

After four days, the control group averaged 43 sit-ups; the treatment group averaged 56 sit-ups.

Does setting specific, measurable goals improve athletic performance?

Hazards in Experimental Design

The Hawthorne Effect — Discovered at the Western Electric plant in 1924: participants who know they are being studied may change their behaviour regardless of the treatment. This makes it difficult to isolate the true effect of the independent variable.

Confounding — Example: subjects placed on a new exercise regimen may simultaneously improve their diet. If health improves, is it exercise, diet, or both? The two factors are confounded.

Unnatural setting — Lab results may not transfer to real-world behaviour (“This mouthwash kills 10,000 germs in a test tube — but how many in my mouth?”).

Section 1–5

Uses and Misuses of Statistics

Statistical methods, applied honestly and rigorously, are among the most powerful tools available to researchers, policy-makers, and citizens. But applied carelessly — or dishonestly — they can mislead just as powerfully.

“There are three kinds of lies: lies, damned lies, and statistics.” — popularised by Mark Twain

“Figures don’t lie, but liars figure.”

The goal of this section is not to make you cynical about statistics, but to sharpen your critical reading of any claim that invokes numerical evidence.

Suspect Samples

Warning: Small or Biased Samples

“Three out of four doctors recommend Brand X.” — If only four doctors were surveyed, this result could easily arise by chance. The key questions to always ask: How large was the sample? and How were subjects selected?

Common sources of sample bias:

  • Volunteer samples — people who self-select into a study tend to have stronger opinions or unusual characteristics not representative of the general population
  • Convenience samples — students in a single classroom, shoppers at one mall, callers to a radio station
  • Online polls — only those who happen to visit a website and feel strongly enough to respond

Ambiguous Averages

There are four distinct measures commonly called an “average”: mean, median, mode, and midrange. For the same data set, these can differ dramatically. A dishonest communicator can choose whichever measure best supports a predetermined conclusion — without technically lying.

# A skewed salary distribution (in $1,000s)
salaries <- c(28, 32, 35, 38, 40, 42, 45, 50, 55, 380)

cat("Salary data ($thousands):", salaries, "\n\n")
Salary data ($thousands): 28 32 35 38 40 42 45 50 55 380 
cat("Mean   :", mean(salaries),   "  ← pulled up by the outlier\n")
Mean   : 74.5   ← pulled up by the outlier
cat("Median :", median(salaries), "  ← resistant to the outlier\n")
Median : 41   ← resistant to the outlier
cat("Mode   : none (all values unique)\n")
Mode   : none (all values unique)
cat("Midrange:", (min(salaries)+max(salaries))/2, "← highly sensitive to extremes\n")
Midrange: 204 ← highly sensitive to extremes

A company with one executive earning $380K and nine staff averaging ~$40K could truthfully advertise an “average salary of $74,500” — technically correct, deeply misleading.

Detached Statistics, Implied Connections, and Misleading Graphs

Warning: Detached Statistics

A detached statistic makes a comparison without naming what it is being compared to. “Our crackers contain one-third fewer calories.” — Fewer than what? Always ask: Compared to what?

Warning: Implied Connections

Hedging language signals that no causal relationship has been established: “may reduce,” “studies suggest,” “in some people,” “might help.” These phrases allow the speaker to retreat from any claim. Read them carefully.

library(ggplot2); library(patchwork)

Faulty Survey Questions

The phrasing of a survey question can dramatically shift responses, even when both versions ask about the same underlying topic.

Example — Question Wording Effect

Version A: “Do you think the North Huntingdon School District should build a new football stadium?”

Version B: “Do you favour increasing school taxes so that the North Huntingdon School District can build a new football stadium?”

Both questions ask about the same proposal. Version B makes the cost explicit, which typically depresses support significantly. The choice of wording can make the same policy appear popular or unpopular.

# Simulating how wording affects support
set.seed(5)
support_A <- rbinom(200, 1, prob = 0.65)  # 65% support (no cost mention)

Section 1–6

Computers and Calculators in Statistics

Before the digital era, statisticians performed all computations by hand — an error-prone and time-consuming process. Modern software has eliminated this bottleneck. This course uses R, a free, open-source language that has become the global standard for statistical analysis and data science.

Key Principle

Software computes; you interpret. R produces numbers. Understanding what those numbers mean, when to trust them, and how to communicate them honestly is the statistician’s job. Doing problems step-by-step by hand first — before turning to software — builds the intuition needed for correct interpretation.

Sampling Error and Repeated Sampling

Example 1-R3: Demonstrating Sampling Error

A population of 500 individuals has a true mean IQ of approximately 100. Draw repeated random samples of size 30 and observe how much sample means vary around the true population mean.

set.seed(2024)
population_iq <- round(rnorm(500, mean = 100, sd = 15))
pop_mean      <- mean(population_iq)

# One sample
one_sample <- sample(population_iq, 30)
cat("Population mean (true) :", round(pop_mean, 2), "\n")
Population mean (true) : 100.76 
cat("One sample mean        :", round(mean(one_sample), 2), "\n")
One sample mean        : 100.47 
cat("Sampling error         :",
    round(abs(mean(one_sample) - pop_mean), 2), "\n")
Sampling error         : 0.29 
set.seed(2024)
pop <- round(rnorm(500, mean = 100, sd = 15))
pop_mean <- mean(pop)

# Repeat sampling 1,000 times
sample_means <- replicate(1000, mean(sample(pop, 30)))

hist(sample_means, breaks = 35, col = "steelblue", border = "white",
     main = "Distribution of 1,000 Sample Means (n = 30)",
     xlab = "Sample Mean", ylab = "Frequency")
abline(v = pop_mean, col = "red", lwd = 2.5, lty = 2)
legend("topright",
       legend = paste("True population mean =", round(pop_mean, 1)),
       col = "red", lty = 2, lwd = 2)

This bell-shaped sampling distribution is a preview of the Central Limit Theorem (Chapter 6): regardless of the population shape, sample means become approximately normally distributed as n grows.

Summary

Chapter 1 Summary

Section 1–1 — Descriptive and Inferential Statistics

  • Descriptive statistics: collect, organise, summarise, present data
  • Inferential statistics: generalise from sample to population using probability
  • Key terms: variable, data, population, sample, random variable

Section 1–2 — Variables and Types of Data

  • Variables are qualitative or quantitative (discrete / continuous)
  • Four measurement scales: nominal → ordinal → interval → ratio
  • Higher levels inherit all properties of lower levels

Section 1–3 — Data Collection and Sampling

  • Main survey methods: telephone, mail, personal interview
  • Four probability sampling strategies: random, systematic, stratified, cluster
  • Convenience sampling is susceptible to bias and should not be used for inference

Section 1–4 — Observational and Experimental Studies

  • Observational: no manipulation → can identify associations, not causes
  • Experimental: researcher controls the independent variable → can support causal claims
  • Watch for: confounding variables, Hawthorne effect, unnatural settings

Section 1–5 — Uses and Misuses of Statistics

  • Misuses include: suspect samples, ambiguous averages, detached statistics, implied connections, misleading graphs, faulty survey questions
  • Critical reading of any statistical claim is essential

Section 1–6 — Computers and Calculators

  • R handles computation; the analyst provides interpretation
  • Core tasks: enter data → sort → summarise → visualise → sample
  • Sampling error is inevitable; understanding its distribution is the foundation of inferential statistics
